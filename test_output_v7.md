Traditional phylogenetic methods rely on the principle of maximum parsimony, which seeks the simplest explanation for determining relationships by minimizing the number of evolutionary changes. By contrast, a Bayesian approach uses a different, probabilistic method that may incorporate the age of fossils (tip dating) into the phylogenetic analysis to estimate divergence times ( 12 ). However, it placed Masripithecus as the stem hominoid that is most closely related to crown hominoids. Al-Ashqar et al. did not analyze the phylogenetic relationship between Masripithecus and Nacholapithecus from Kenya (15 Ma), which has been interpreted as either a stem hominoid ( 10 ) or a hominid ( 13 ). Moreover, the authors’ maximum-parsimony and Bayesian analyses that did not incorporate the age of fossils resulted in different phylogenetic tree topologies than the tip-dating analysis, as they placed Masripithecus as a crown (instead of a stem) hominoid. Future phylogenetic analyses that include Nacholapithecus and use other datasets are required to conclusively discount a crown hominoid status for Masripithecus . Inflammation is transient, but its consequences can be lifelong. Even brief episodes of inflammation can lead to long-lasting changes in chromatin that control gene expression. These changes can poise cells to respond differently to subsequent inflammation episodes. For example, an initial encounter with environmental stressors, such as bacteria, viruses, or Western diets, durably tunes innate immune responses to later (related or unrelated) challenges ( 1 , 2 ). These mechanisms also exist in nonimmune cells, including the epidermal cells of human skin ( 3 , 4 ). Why some inflammation-induced chromatin alterations are transient whereas others persist is unclear. On page 1347 of this issue, Cowley et al . ( 5 ) report that specific regions dense in cytosine and guanosine dinucleotides (CpG-rich) are epigenetically modified during inflammation to enable gene expression and that these changes persist during the animal’s lifetime. The finding has implications for understanding how the genome determines the longevity of memory, which affects tissue fitness. Epigenetic alterations to chromatin (the architecture of DNA wrapped around histone proteins) affect gene expression without modifying the DNA sequence. These modifications can change the conformation of chromatin between open (accessible to transcription factors) and closed states. Cowley et al . applied imiquimod to the skin of mice to induce psoriasis-like inflammation, which self-resolves within 30 days. This transient perturbation allowed the authors to distinguish between chromatin changes that fade during the resolution of inflammation and those that persist long after inflammation has ended. Cowley et al . defined “long-term memory domains” as genomic regions that were more open in inflamed skin compared with age-matched naïve skin at day 30 and whose genomic accessibility persisted 1 year after inflammation. Notably, most of these regions remained open for up to 2 years (a common lifetime for a mouse) after inflammation. Conversely, “short-term memory domains” closed up between day 30 and year 1. Single-cell profiling revealed that long-lasting genomic memory is broadly distributed across the epidermal stem cell population rather than being confined to a niche-restricted subset. The persistence of epigenetic alterations has been primarily explained through mechanisms that regulate gene expression. Chromatin states induced by a specific stimulus are sustained after its withdrawal through transcription factor binding, cofactor recruitment, and chromatin remodeling, which are reinforced by feedback loops within regulatory circuits ( 6 , 7 ). In this framework, the genome specifies where regulators can act, whereas the longevity of epigenetic alterations results chiefly from how long these regulatory processes can maintain a chromatin configuration that permits transcription. Cowley et al . refined the hierarchy of mechanisms underlying inflammatory memory by parsing out establishment from persistence. During the initial inflammatory response in the mouse model of psoriasis, the protein signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) and the activator protein–1 (AP-1) transcription factor helped to open memory domains.
