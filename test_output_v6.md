Traditional phylogenetic methods rely on the principle of maximum parsimony, which seeks the simplest explanation for determining relationships by minimizing the number of evolutionary changes. By contrast, a Bayesian approach uses adifferent, probabilistic method that may incorporate the age of fossils (tip dating) into the phylogenetic analysis to estimate divergence times ( 12 ). However, it placed Masripithecus as the stem hominoid that is most closely related to crown hominoids. Al-Ashqar et al. did not analyze the phylogenetic relationship between Masripithecus and Nacholapithecus from Kenya (15 Ma), which has been interpreted as either astem hominoid ( 10 ) or ahominid ( 13 ). Moreover, the authors’ maximum-parsimony and Bayesian analyses that did not incorporate the age of fossils resulted in different phylogenetic tree topologies than the tip-dating analysis, as they placed Masripithecus as acrown (instead of astem) hominoid. Future phylogenetic analyses that include Nacholapithecus and use other datasets are required to conclusively discount acrown hominoid status for Masripithecus . Inflammation is transient, but its consequences can be lifelong. Even brief episodes of inflammation can lead to long-lasting changes in chromatin that control gene expression. These changes can poise cells to respond differently to subsequent inflammation episodes. For example, an initial encounter with environmental stressors, such as bacteria, viruses, or Western diets, durably tunes innate immune responses to later (related or unrelated) challenges ( 1 , 2 ). These mechanisms also exist in nonimmune cells, including the epidermal cells of human skin ( 3 , 4 ). Why some inflammation-induced chromatin alterations are transient whereas others persist is unclear. On page 1347 of this issue, Cowley et al . ( 5 ) report that specific regions dense in cytosine and guanosine dinucleotides (CpG-rich) are epigenetically modified during inflammation to enable gene expression and that these changes persist during the animal’s lifetime. The finding has implications for understanding how the genome determines the longevity of memory, which affects tissue fitness. Epigenetic alterations to chromatin (the architecture of DNA wrapped around histone proteins) affect gene expression without modifying the DNA sequence. These modifications can change the conformation of chromatin between open (accessible to transcription factors) and closed states. Cowley et al . applied imiquimod to the skin of mice to induce psoriasis-like inflammation, which self-resolves within 30 days. This transient perturbation allowed the authors to distinguish between chromatin changes that fade during the resolution of inflammation and those that persist long after inflammation has ended. Cowley et al . defined “long-term memory domains” as genomic regions that were more open in inflamed skin compared with age-matched naïve skin at day 30 and whose genomic accessibility persisted 1 year after inflammation. Notably, most of these regions remained open for up to 2 years (a common lifetime for amouse) after inflammation. Conversely, “short-term memory domains” closed up between day 30 and year 1. Single-cell profiling revealed that long-lasting genomic memory is broadly distributed across the epidermal stem cell population rather than being confined to aniche-restricted subset. The persistence of epigenetic alterations has been primarily explained through mechanisms that regulate gene expression. Chromatin states induced by aspecific stimulus are sustained after its withdrawal through transcription factor binding, cofactor recruitment, and chromatin remodeling, which are reinforced by feedback loops within regulatory circuits ( 6 , 7 ). In this framework, the genome specifies where regulators can act, whereas the longevity of epigenetic alterations results chiefly from how long these regulatory processes can maintain achromatin configuration that permits transcription. Cowley et al . refined the hierarchy of mechanisms underlying inflammatory memory by parsing out establishment from persistence. During the initial inflammatory response in the mouse model of psoriasis, the protein signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) and the activator protein–1 (AP-1) transcription factor helped to open memory domains. 此外，社会通常只关注量化自然灾害的直接经济损失，而忽视其他效应如社会不平等、心理健康问题及人口迁移等。因此，与连锁反应相关的损害综合估算仍难以获得。那么如何追踪、监测、预测并理解级联影响的空间范围和时间关系？建立可跟踪这些级联事件的全球监控系统将有助于开发关键资源数据库以深入分析和更好地理解此类事件。该信息可用于风险管理和引起对其他被忽视反馈过程的关注。数据还可促进更稳健的信息发展，提供关于自然灾害及其全球尺度后果的见解。通过多轮次信息交换实现模型双向耦合可增进危害、人类活动及跨部门影响之间相互作用的理解。最终，在考虑级联效应、反馈环路及其复杂互动时应有助于未来严重事件准备和气候极端值间快速转变或"打摆子"现象的应对。炎症是短暂性的，但其后果可能伴随终生。即使短时间的炎症也可能导致染色质持久性改变从而调控基因表达。这些变化可使细胞对后续炎症发作产生不同反应。例如初次接触环境应激源如细菌、病毒或西方饮食会永久调节先天免疫响应以应对后来（相关或无关）挑战(1, 2)。此类机制也存在于非免疫细胞中，包括人类皮肤表皮细胞(3,4)。为何某些炎症诱导的染色质改变是短暂性的而另一些则持久存在尚不清楚。在本期第1347页，Cowley等人（5）报告称特定富含胞嘧啶和鸟嘌呤二核苷酸（CpG-rich区域）在炎症期间通过表观遗传修饰以实现基因表达，并且这些改变会持续动物的整个生命周期。这一发现对理解基因组如何决定记忆持久性从而影响组织适应力具有重要意义。染色质（DNA缠绕组蛋白形成的结构）的表观遗传改变会影响基因表达而不修改DNA序列。这些变化可使染色质在开放状态（转录因子可访问）与闭合状态之间转换。Cowley等人将咪喹莫特应用于小鼠皮肤以诱导银屑病样炎症，该炎症会在30天内自行缓解。这种短暂扰动使得作者能够区分炎症消退期间消失的染色质变化以及在炎症结束后长期持续的变化。Cowley等人定义"长时记忆区域"为在第30天与同龄未受刺激皮肤相比更开放且其基因组可及性维持1年后的炎性皮肤区域。值得注意的是，大多数这些区域在炎症后可持续保持开放达2年（小鼠常见寿命）。相反，"短时记忆区域"则在第30天至第一年后关闭。单细胞分析显示长持续的基因组记忆广泛分布于表皮干细胞群体而非局限于特定微环境子集。表观遗传改变的持久性主要通过调控基因表达机制进行解释。由特异性刺激诱导的染色质状态在其撤除后可通过转录因子结合、共调节因子招募及染色质重塑维持，这些过程受调控回路内的反馈环强化(6,7)。在此框架中，基因组指定调控因子可作用的位置，而表观遗传改变的持久性主要取决于这些调控过程能维持何种染色质构型以允许转录的时间长度。Cowley等人通过区分建立与持续机制细化了炎症记忆的基础理论体系。在银屑病小鼠模型初始炎性反应中蛋白信号传导子和激活因子3（STAT3）及活化蛋白-1（AP-1）转录因子帮助打开记忆区域。这一过程伴随这些记忆区域内组蛋白H3的甲基化与乙酰化（分别对应H3K4me1和H3K27ac表观遗传改变）。然而，这些机制无法解释炎症记忆的持久性因为部分开放区域随后关闭而另一些则保持可访问状态数年。
